Informed Consent

User Perceptions of Trust and Transparency in Music Recommendation Explanations

Introduction

You are invited to participate in a research study conducted as part of a Master's thesis in User Experience Design at Jönköping University, Sweden. The purpose of this study is to investigate how different types of explanations influence users' transparency and trust in music recommendations.

What does participation involve?

You will interact with a music recommendation prototype, select one song you like, and view recommendations with explanations. You will answer a short questionnaire after each interaction. The study takes approximately 10 minutes.

Who can participate?

  • Be 18 years or older
  • Be familiar with music streaming platforms (e.g., Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music)

Voluntary Participation

Participation is completely voluntary. You may stop or withdraw at any time without providing a reason.

Data Protection

All data is anonymous and used only for academic research. No personally identifiable information will be collected. Results will be reported in a master's thesis, but individual participants will not be identifiable.

Instructions

How this study works

You will interact with a prototype of a music recommendation interface. Your task is to explore how the system recommends songs and explains its recommendations.

Practice round first

You will start with a practice version to get familiar with the interface. In this step you select one song and view recommendations, no explanations yet, and no questionnaire.

Then the real tasks

1

Select one song you like from a list.

2

The system shows recommended songs.

3

Each recommended song includes an explanation describing why it was recommended.

4

Read the explanations carefully.

5

Answer a short questionnaire about your experience.

This process is repeated a few times with slightly different explanation styles.

Important: Please pay attention to the explanations. You will be asked questions about them after each round.

The study takes approximately 10 minutes.
